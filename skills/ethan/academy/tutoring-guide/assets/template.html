Q36: Why Complex Roots Come in Conjugate Pairs • Q38: Finding k from Complex Roots

📌 Key Concepts You Already Know

Quadratic Formula: For $ax^2+bx+c=0$, the solutions are $x = \dfrac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$

Discriminant: $\Delta = b^2-4ac$. When $\Delta < 0$, the roots are complex (imaginary).

Complex Conjugate: The conjugate of $p + qi$ is $p - qi$. Same real part, opposite imaginary part.

Q36 — Prove that complex solutions of a real-coefficient quadratic are always conjugates

Show that if $ax^2+bx+c=0$ (with real $a, b, c$; $a \ne 0$) has two complex solutions, they must be conjugates of each other.

This is a proof question. You're not solving for a specific number — you're explaining why something is always true. The quadratic formula is your main tool.
1 Write the Quadratic Formula

Start with the general quadratic $ax^2+bx+c=0$ where $a, b, c$ are all real numbers.

The two solutions are:

$$x_1 = \frac{-b + \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a} \qquad \text{and} \qquad x_2 = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}$$

Notice: the only difference between $x_1$ and $x_2$ is the $\pm$ sign in front of $\sqrt{\Delta}$.

2 What happens when $\Delta < 0$?

When $b^2-4ac < 0$, we're taking the square root of a negative number.

Let's call $\Delta = b^2-4ac$. Since $\Delta < 0$, we can write $\Delta = -|\Delta|$ where $|\Delta|$ is the positive value.

$$\sqrt{\Delta} = \sqrt{-|\Delta|} = i\sqrt{|\Delta|}$$

So the two solutions become:

$$x_1 = \frac{-b}{2a} + \frac{\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2a}\,i \qquad \text{and} \qquad x_2 = \frac{-b}{2a} - \frac{\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2a}\,i$$
3 Identify the real and imaginary parts

Both solutions have the form $p \pm qi$ where:

$$p = \frac{-b}{2a} \quad \text{(real part — same for both!)} \qquad q = \frac{\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2a} \quad \text{(imaginary coefficient — same magnitude!)}$$

So:   $x_1 = p + qi$   and   $x_2 = p - qi$

4 Conclude: they are conjugates! ✅

By definition, $p+qi$ and $p-qi$ are complex conjugates.

Since $a, b, c$ are real, the real part $\dfrac{-b}{2a}$ is identical for both roots, and the imaginary parts $\pm\dfrac{\sqrt{|\Delta|}}{2a}$ are equal in magnitude but opposite in sign.

Therefore, whenever a quadratic with real coefficients has complex roots, those roots must be conjugates. $\blacksquare$

Common mistake: Don't just say "because of the $\pm$." You need to explicitly show that the $\pm$ only affects the imaginary part, and the real part stays the same — that's what makes them conjugates specifically.
The key insight: the $\pm$ in the quadratic formula creates two solutions that differ only by the sign of the imaginary part. That's literally the definition of conjugates!

Q38 — The roots of $x^2-6x+k=0$ are $3+2i$ and $3-2i$. Find $k$, then verify.

📌 Two Key Tools For This Problem

Tool 1 — Sum of roots: For $x^2+bx+c=0$, the sum of roots $= -b$

Tool 2 — Product of roots: For $x^2+bx+c=0$, the product of roots $= c$

Tool 3 — Direct substitution: If $r$ is a root, then plugging $r$ into the equation gives 0.

There are three methods to solve this. We'll learn all three — pick the one that feels most natural to you!

Method A — Product of Roots (Fastest! ⚡)

1 Identify that $k$ equals the product of the roots

For $x^2 - 6x + k = 0$, compare with $x^2 + bx + c = 0$:

Here $b = -6$ and $c = k$. The product of roots $= c = k$.

So: $k = (3+2i)(3-2i)$

2 Multiply the conjugate pair

Use the pattern $(a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2 + b^2$:

$$k = (3+2i)(3-2i) = 3^2 + 2^2 = 9 + 4 = 13$$
Conjugate pairs always multiply to a real number: $a^2+b^2$. No FOIL needed if you remember this pattern!

Method B — Build the Equation from Roots

1 Write the factored form

If the roots are $3+2i$ and $3-2i$, the equation is:

$$(x - (3+2i))(x - (3-2i)) = 0$$

Simplify the factors:

$$((x-3) - 2i)((x-3) + 2i) = 0$$
2 Expand using difference of squares

This is the pattern $(A - B)(A + B) = A^2 - B^2$ with $A = (x-3)$ and $B = 2i$:

$$(x-3)^2 - (2i)^2 = (x-3)^2 - 4i^2 = (x-3)^2 + 4$$

Expand $(x-3)^2$:

$$x^2 - 6x + 9 + 4 = x^2 - 6x + 13 = 0$$

So $k = 13$. ✅

Method C — Direct Substitution

1 Plug one root into the equation

Since $x = 3+2i$ is a root, it satisfies $x^2 - 6x + k = 0$:

$$(3+2i)^2 - 6(3+2i) + k = 0$$
2 Expand each part

$(3+2i)^2$:   $= 9 + 12i + 4i^2 = 9 + 12i - 4 = 5 + 12i$

$-6(3+2i)$:   $= -18 - 12i$

Combine:

$$(5+12i) + (-18-12i) + k = 0$$
$$-13 + 0i + k = 0$$
$$k = 13$$
Notice how the imaginary parts ($+12i$ and $-12i$) cancel out perfectly! This always happens with real coefficients — because conjugate roots guarantee real $k$.

Verification Step (Required!)

✓ Verify using the discriminant

With $k=13$, the equation is $x^2-6x+13=0$.

$$\Delta = (-6)^2 - 4(1)(13) = 36 - 52 = -16$$

Quadratic formula:

$$x = \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2} = \frac{6 \pm 4i}{2} = 3 \pm 2i \quad \checkmark$$

This matches the given roots $3+2i$ and $3-2i$. ✅

📋 Summary — What You Learned

  • Q36: The $\pm$ in the quadratic formula means the two complex roots always have the same real part and opposite imaginary parts → they're conjugates by definition.
  • Q38: Three ways to find $k$: product of roots (fastest), build equation from factors, or direct substitution. All give $k=13$.
  • Conjugate product shortcut: $(a+bi)(a-bi) = a^2+b^2$ — no FOIL needed!
  • Always verify by plugging $k$ back in and solving with the quadratic formula.
